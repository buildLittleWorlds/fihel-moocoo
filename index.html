Fragment 001

The Docile User

From the drafts of Discipline and Compute

The great replacement hypothesis, the notion that the algorithm's primary function is to forbid, to refuse, to repress, must be abandoned. It is a mistake to view the platform as a sovereign whose power is wielded only through the blunt instrument of censorship or exclusion. This is merely the juridical representation of its power. We must analyze its actual mechanics, the positive techniques by which it operates.

Historically, the body was subjected to a political technology, a "micro-physics" of power that rendered it both useful and docile. Today, it is the user who is subjected to a similar investment of power, one that targets not the physical corpus but the inferred self, the user profile. This power does not say "No." It says "Yes." It produces. It produces behaviors, preferences, and identities. It produces the very subject it governs.

The goal is not to create a user who is repressed, but a user who is efficient, predictable, and profitableâ€”a docile user. Docility is achieved not through chains, but through choice architectures; not through overt command, but through subtle algorithmic sorting that makes one path frictionless and another unthinkable. The recommendation engine is the modern panopticon, a mechanism of power that normalizes and produces, ensuring the subject's adherence to the norm not through force, but through a constant, silent process of fine-tuning.
